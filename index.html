Introduction

This report presents the implementation and performance comparison of three different maximal clique detection algorithms:

  1. Tomita et al.'s Algorithm (2006)
  2. Bron-Kerbosch Algorithm with Pivoting (1973)
  3. Arboricity-based Clique Algorithm (Recent optimizations using degeneracy ordering)

These algorithms were tested on three real-world graph datasets:

  • Email-Enron Dataset - Communication network of Enron employees.
  • AS-Skitter Dataset - Network topology of the Internet at the autonomous systems (AS) level.
  • Wiki-Vote Dataset - Voting relationships between Wikipedia editors.

The goal of this study is to analyze execution time, the number of maximal cliques, and the distribution of clique sizes, while comparing the efficiency of different algorithmic approaches.

Research Paper 1: Tomita et al.'s Algorithm

Brief Description

Tomita et al. developed an enhanced branch-and-bound method for maximal clique detection. Their algorithm incorporates pivoting to minimize unnecessary recursive calls, outperforming the traditional Bron-Kerbosch method.

Algorithm Overview

The process involves selecting a pivot vertex that maximizes overlap with potential vertices, and then expanding the clique by recursively adding non-neighboring nodes of the pivot. The algorithm backtracks when no more candidate vertices are available.

Research Paper 2: Bron-Kerbosch Algorithm with Pivoting

Brief Description

The Bron-Kerbosch algorithm is a recursive backtracking method for finding maximal cliques. We implemented the pivoting version, which improves performance by minimizing the number of recursive calls.

Algorithm Overview

Select a pivot vertex (reduces the branching factor). Recursively explore clique extensions by including/excluding vertices. Backtrack and prune search space when no further clique extensions exist. This algorithm is widely used due to its simplicity and efficiency for small to medium-sized graphs.

Research Paper 3: Arboricity-based Clique Algorithm

Brief Description

The Arboricity-based clique enumeration algorithm, developed by Chiba and Nishizeki, utilizes a graph's arboricity to efficiently find cliques. It systematically scans edges, processing vertices by decreasing degree and examining neighbor-induced subgraphs.

Algorithm Overview

The algorithm runs in O(a(G) * m) time per clique, where a(G) is the arboricity and m is the number of edges. This approach excels in graphs with low arboricity, such as planar or sparse networks, outperforming traditional methods. However, it may be less effective in dense graphs with high arboricity, like social networks, where degeneracy-based methods like Bron-Kerbosch with pivoting might be more efficient.

Conclusion

Maximal clique detection identifies fully connected vertex subsets in undirected graphs, with applications in social networks and cybersecurity. This study evaluates three algorithms: Tomita et al. (2006), Bron-Kerbosch with Pivoting (1973), and the Arboricity-based Clique Algorithm.

Tested on Email-Enron, AS-Skitter, and Wiki-Vote datasets, the analysis measures execution time, total cliques, and clique size distributions. Key findings include:

  • Bron-Kerbosch remains the most efficient choice for maximal clique detection, excelling in both execution time and accuracy.
  • Tomita is a competitive alternative, performing similarly to Bron-Kerbosch with only minor performance trade-offs.
  • The Arboricity-based approach is inefficient and unsuitable for large-scale real-world networks.
  • AS-Skitter demonstrates the scalability challenges in clique detection, requiring impractically long computation times.
  • Wiki-Vote and Email-Enron highlight different network structures, where Wiki-Vote favors mid-sized cliques, while Email-Enron contains larger but fewer cliques.
